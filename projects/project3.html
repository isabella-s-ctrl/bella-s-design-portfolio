Plaid Pickup – Improving Package Pickup Efficiency at CMU

Plaid Pickup was a service design and UX project aimed at streamlining Carnegie Mellon University's package pickup process by improving queue transparency and reducing student wait times.

Role: UX/UI Designer (Business Technology Group team project) Timeline: 1 semester (Fall 2024–Spring 2025) Tools: Figma, Miro, Google Suite

overview

This project was conducted through CMU's Business Technology Group (BTG) Product Studio, where I worked as part of a team to address a real campus service challenge.

The campus mailroom struggled with long lines and inefficient communication about wait times, creating frustration for both students and staff. Our goal was to increase efficiency in the pickup process, provide transparency about queue length and wait times, and improve the student user experience while reducing operational bottlenecks.

Efficient package pickup is a high-frequency, high-frustration task for students. Improving this service directly impacts student satisfaction and campus operations, making it a meaningful project with real-world impact.

the problem

Long wait times and lack of real-time updates created significant frustration and inefficiency in the campus mailroom. Students had no clear sense of how long they would wait, staff had no structured way to communicate or manage throughput, and the experience varied dramatically depending on day and time.

The core challenge was creating a solution that balanced simplicity for students with scalability for campus operations, while addressing the fundamental need for reliable, transparent information about line length, wait time, and pickup progress.

Scope Shifting

We had to pivot after realizing our initial idea wasn't feasible given BTG's constraints and available resources.

Limited Engagement

By the end of the year, mailroom staff were less invested, making long-term implementation difficult.

Data Collection Barriers

Measuring line metrics consistently (packages, wait time, throughput) required operational support that we couldn't fully secure.

my process

1. research

I conducted observational research in the mailroom to map current workflows and identify key metrics like package count, line size, and average pickup time. I also interviewed students to understand their frustrations and expectations with the current system.

2. ideation

I brainstormed ways to reduce uncertainty, including digital queue management and real-time wait estimates. I sketched potential UI flows for package acknowledgment and wait-time displays that would improve transparency.

3. prototyping

I created low- to mid-fidelity wireframes in Figma showing how students could check in and see expected wait times. I designed UI for acknowledging package quantity and entering the queue system.

4. final design

I developed a concept for a self-service kiosk/scan system where students input package count and receive estimated wait times, with real-time visibility into queue progress via mobile or on-site displays.

the solution

Plaid Pickup is a digital line management and wait-time transparency system designed to streamline the package pickup process. The solution addresses the core challenges through thoughtful service design and user-centered interface development.

Queue Acknowledgment

Students press a button to confirm they're in line, providing immediate feedback and reducing uncertainty about their place in the queue.

Package Count Input

Students enter how many packages they're picking up, improving throughput predictions and helping staff prepare accordingly.

Wait-Time Display

Real-time visibility shows how many people are ahead and estimated wait times, giving students clear expectations.

Mobile Integration

Queue progress accessible via mobile devices or on-site displays, providing flexibility for different user preferences.

results & impact

The project produced a validated concept and UI designs, though it was not fully implemented due to scope changes and reduced stakeholder engagement. However, early student feedback showed strong interest in greater transparency during pickup, and our research provided valuable metrics for potential future campus service improvements.

Average Pickup Time

Varied significantly by package count, with 1-2 packages being most common per student

Line Throughput Capacity

Identified optimal capacity based on physical space constraints and staff availability

Package Quantity Patterns

Majority of students (70%) had 1-2 packages, informing queue management strategy

Key Learnings

  • Importance of scoping realistically with stakeholder buy-in from the beginning
  • Even if a project doesn't launch, strong research and design artifacts can still demonstrate process and value
  • Communication and continuity with clients is critical to prevent projects from losing momentum
